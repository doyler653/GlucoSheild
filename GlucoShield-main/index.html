🔬 What is Blood Glucose?

Blood glucose (also called blood sugar) is the amount of glucose in your bloodstream. Glucose is a simple sugar that your body uses for energy. It's absolutely essential for survival, but keeping levels within a healthy range is crucial for long-term health and preventing serious complications.

Normal Glucose Ranges

🎯 Fasting (before eating)

4.0–7.0 mmol/L

This is the normal range when you haven't eaten for 8+ hours.

🍽️ After Meals

Less than 8.0 mmol/L

Your glucose rises temporarily after eating, then returns to normal as your body processes the food.

⚖️ Throughout the Day

3.9–7.8 mmol/L (average)

Your glucose naturally fluctuates based on meals, activity, stress, and sleep.

When Glucose is Too Low (Hypoglycemia)

⚠️ < 4.0 mmol/L

Symptoms: Dizziness, trembling, sweating, confusion, weakness, rapid heartbeat

Risk: Can lead to seizures or loss of consciousness if not treated quickly

Immediate Treatment: Fast-acting carbs (glucose tablets, juice, candy)

When Glucose is Too High (Hyperglycemia)

⚠️ > 10.0 mmol/L

Symptoms: Increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision

Risk: Long-term high glucose damages blood vessels and nerves, leading to heart disease, kidney problems, and vision loss

Management: Balanced diet, exercise, medical treatment if needed

🍽️ How Food Affects Blood Glucose

When you eat carbohydrates, your digestive system breaks them down into glucose molecules. These glucose molecules are absorbed into your bloodstream, which is why eating raises your blood glucose. Different types of carbohydrates are broken down at different speeds, which is why some foods cause rapid glucose spikes while others cause gradual increases.

Carbohydrates in Different Foods

🍎

Apple (medium)

~15g carbs | Raises BG by ~1.5 mmol/L | Contains fiber (slower absorption)

🍞

Bread Slice (white or whole wheat)

10–30g carbs | Varies by bread type | White bread ↑ glucose faster than whole wheat

🍭

Candy/Sweet

~25g carbs | Raises BG very quickly (high glycemic index) | Mostly simple sugars, minimal fiber

🥗

Vegetables & Lean Protein

Low carbs | Minimal impact on BG | Fiber slows glucose absorption significantly

Glycemic Index (GI)

The Glycemic Index measures how quickly a food raises blood glucose on a scale of 0-100:

🔴 High GI (>70)

White bread, candy, sugary drinks, white rice

🟡 Medium GI (56-69)

Whole wheat bread, brown rice, oats

🟢 Low GI (<55)

Beans, nuts, most vegetables, whole grains

💉 Insulin: The Key Regulator

Insulin is a hormone produced by your pancreas (a gland below your stomach). It acts like a "key" that opens cells to allow them to absorb glucose from the bloodstream. Without insulin, glucose can't enter cells efficiently, so it accumulates in the blood.

How Insulin Works

The Insulin Cycle

  1. You eat carbs → Glucose enters bloodstream
  2. Glucose rises → Pancreas detects increase
  3. Pancreas releases insulin → Insulin circulates in blood
  4. Insulin "unlocks" cells → Cells absorb glucose
  5. Glucose decreases → Pancreas stops releasing insulin
  6. Cycle returns to normal → Ready for next meal

What Insulin Does

✅ Lowers Blood Glucose

Allows cells to absorb glucose for energy or storage

✅ Stores Energy

Unused glucose is stored as glycogen or fat for later use

✅ Regulates Metabolism

Helps your body process carbs, fats, and proteins efficiently

🩺 Diabetes: When Glucose Control Breaks Down

Diabetes occurs when the body can't properly regulate blood glucose levels, either because it can't produce insulin or can't use it effectively.

Type 1 Diabetes

What Happens

The immune system attacks the cells in the pancreas that produce insulin. The pancreas stops making insulin completely.

Who Gets It

Usually diagnosed in children and young adults (autoimmune condition)

Management

Insulin therapy: Daily insulin injections or insulin pump | Carb counting: Track and balance carbs | Blood testing: Regular glucose monitoring | Lifestyle: Healthy diet and exercise

Type 2 Diabetes

What Happens

The body produces insulin, but cells become resistant to it. Glucose can't enter cells effectively, so it accumulates in the blood (insulin resistance).

Who Gets It

Usually develops in adults (age 45+), but increasingly common in younger people. Risk factors: obesity, family history, sedentary lifestyle

Management

Lifestyle changes: Diet and exercise (often enough to control) | Medication: Oral medications if lifestyle changes aren't enough | Insulin: Sometimes needed in advanced cases

Prediabetes

A warning stage where blood glucose is higher than normal but not high enough to be diagnosed as type 2 diabetes. The good news: prediabetes can be reversed with lifestyle changes before it becomes full diabetes.

💪 Prevention & Management Strategies

Whether you want to prevent diabetes or manage existing diabetes, the same healthy habits apply:

🥗 Eat Healthy

Emphasize: Whole grains, vegetables, lean proteins, healthy fats

Limit: Sugary drinks, candy, refined carbs, processed foods

Tip: Pair carbs with protein and fiber to slow glucose absorption

🏃 Exercise Regularly

Goal: 150 minutes moderate activity per week

Why: Exercise makes cells more sensitive to insulin

Bonus: Even a 10-minute walk after meals helps lower glucose spikes

⚖️ Maintain Healthy Weight

Why: Excess fat tissue increases insulin resistance

Impact: Even 5-10% weight loss can improve insulin sensitivity significantly

😴 Sleep Well

Goal: 7-9 hours per night

Impact: Poor sleep reduces insulin sensitivity

Connection: Sleep deprivation increases stress hormones that raise glucose

🧘 Manage Stress

How: Meditation, yoga, deep breathing, hobbies

Why: Stress hormones increase glucose production

📊 Monitor Regularly

Fasting glucose: Check occasionally to track trends

A1C test: Average glucose over 3 months (diabetes screening)

Why: Early detection prevents complications

🎓 Key Takeaways

Balance is Key

Maintaining healthy blood glucose requires balance between food intake, physical activity, stress, sleep, and insulin production. It's a whole-body system.

Not All Carbs Are Equal

Complex carbs with fiber raise glucose more slowly than simple sugars. Choose whole grains, vegetables, and legumes.

Early Detection Saves Lives

Regular glucose monitoring and lifestyle changes can prevent or delay type 2 diabetes by decades.

It's About More Than Sugar

Stress, sleep, exercise, and weight all affect how your body handles glucose. True health requires a holistic approach.

You Have Control

Most cases of prediabetes and type 2 diabetes can be managed or prevented through lifestyle choices you make every day.

Knowledge is Power

Understanding how your body works gives you the tools to make informed decisions about your health.

👩‍🏫 Diabetes Educator Page

This page is designed to support teachers and diabetes educators using Glucose Quest and DiaCare Adventure as educational tools. It outlines the learning intentions and learning outcomes linked to the activities in the game and accompanying content.

🎯 Learning Intentions

Students will learn...

what blood glucose is and why it is important for health.

Students will learn...

how carbohydrates affect blood glucose levels.

Students will learn...

the role of insulin in regulating blood glucose.

Students will learn...

the difference between healthy glucose balance, hypoglycaemia and hyperglycaemia.

Students will learn...

how lifestyle choices such as diet, exercise, sleep and stress affect glucose control.

Students will learn...

how interactive simulation and gameplay can help develop scientific understanding.

✅ Learning Outcomes

By the end of this activity, students should be able to:

define blood glucose and explain its role in the body.

By the end of this activity, students should be able to:

describe how different foods can raise blood glucose levels by different amounts.

By the end of this activity, students should be able to:

explain how insulin helps lower blood glucose by allowing cells to absorb glucose.

By the end of this activity, students should be able to:

identify the symptoms and risks of glucose levels that are too low or too high.

By the end of this activity, students should be able to:

compare type 1 diabetes, type 2 diabetes and prediabetes in simple terms.

By the end of this activity, students should be able to:

suggest practical ways to support healthy glucose regulation and diabetes prevention.

🧩 Suggested Classroom Use

Starter: Ask students what they already know about blood sugar and diabetes.

Main activity: Students play Glucose Quest and/or DiaCare Adventure.

Discussion: Pause to discuss the effect of apples, bread, sweets and insulin on glucose levels.

Reflection: Students explain what they learned about keeping blood glucose in a healthy range.

Extension: Students design their own health game level or infographic based on glucose balance.
